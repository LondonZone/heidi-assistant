CROUP CLINICAL PRACTICE GUIDELINE
Royal Children's Hospital Melbourne

DEFINITION
Croup (laryngotracheobronchitis) is a viral upper respiratory tract infection causing subglottic inflammation. Characterised by barky cough, hoarse voice, inspiratory stridor, and variable respiratory distress. Most commonly parainfluenza virus.

SEVERITY — WESTLEY CROUP SCORE
Level of consciousness: Normal (0), Altered (5)
Cyanosis: None (0), With agitation (4), At rest (5)
Stridor: None (0), With agitation (1), At rest (2)
Air entry: Normal (0), Decreased (1), Markedly decreased (2)
Retractions: None (0), Mild (1), Moderate (2), Severe (3)

Score 0-2: Mild | Score 3-7: Moderate | Score 8+: Severe

MANAGEMENT

MILD (Westley 0-2):
- Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg oral, single dose, max 10mg
- Reassure, discharge with return-to-ED instructions

MODERATE (Westley 3-7):
- Dexamethasone 0.30 mg/kg oral, single dose, max 10mg
- Observe 2 hours minimum, reassess at 30 min and 2 hr
- Consider nebulised adrenaline 0.5ml/kg (max 5ml) 1:1000 if not improving

SEVERE (Westley 8+):
- Dexamethasone 0.60 mg/kg IV/IM, single dose, max 10mg
- Nebulised adrenaline 0.5ml/kg (max 5ml) 1:1000 IMMEDIATELY
- High-flow oxygen if SpO2 <92%
- Senior clinician review, ICU notification
- Minimum 4 hour observation

DISCHARGE CRITERIA (Moderate)
- No stridor at rest for 2 hours
- Normal work of breathing
- Tolerating oral fluids
- SpO2 >95% on room air
- Parents confident, written return instructions provided

DANGEROUS DIFFERENTIALS TO EXCLUDE
- Epiglottitis: toxic, drooling, tripod, muffled voice
- Bacterial tracheitis: high fever, toxic, purulent secretions
- Foreign body: sudden onset, no prodrome, unilateral wheeze
- Retropharyngeal abscess: unilateral swelling, trismus
