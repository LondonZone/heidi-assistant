CROUP — NICE CLINICAL KNOWLEDGE SUMMARY
National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (UK)

DEFINITION
Croup is a viral laryngotracheobronchitis with characteristic barking cough, hoarse voice, and inspiratory stridor.

SEVERITY ASSESSMENT
NICE recommends clinical severity assessment based on:
- Stridor (none, with activity, at rest)
- Recession (none, mild, moderate, severe)
- Air entry (normal, decreased, markedly decreased)
- General appearance (well, distressed, lethargic)
- Cyanosis (none, pallor, cyanosis)

MILD: Seal-like barking cough, no stridor or recession at rest
MODERATE: Stridor and recession at rest, no agitation
SEVERE: Stridor at rest with marked recession, agitation, tachycardia

MANAGEMENT

MILD:
- Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg oral, single dose
- OR Prednisolone 1 mg/kg oral, single dose (if dexamethasone unavailable)
- Reassure, provide written safety-netting advice

MODERATE:
- Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg oral, single dose
- Consider observation in ED for 2-4 hours
- Nebulised adrenaline 400 micrograms/kg (max 5mg) of 1:1000 if severe symptoms develop

SEVERE:
- URGENT — 999 if community presentation
- Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg oral OR 0.6 mg/kg IM if unable to tolerate oral
- Nebulised adrenaline 400 micrograms/kg (max 5mg) 1:1000 IMMEDIATELY
- High-flow oxygen, pulse oximetry
- Paediatric referral, consider ICU

NOTE ON UK DOSING
NICE uses 0.15 mg/kg dexamethasone across severity levels as standard. Higher IM dose (0.6 mg/kg) reserved for severe cases where oral route not feasible.

DISCHARGE
Can discharge from ED after:
- Stridor-free at rest for minimum 2-4 hours post-treatment
- No tachypnoea or respiratory distress
- Parents able to recognise deterioration
- Clear safety-netting advice provided

DANGEROUS DIFFERENTIALS
Epiglottitis, bacterial tracheitis, foreign body aspiration, anaphylaxis.
